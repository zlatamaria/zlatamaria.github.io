Maria Marina

I am a third-year PhD student in Computer Science at Skoltech, advised by Alexander Panchenko. Since February 2024, I have also been a Research Fellow at AIRI, where I am part of the Computational Semantics Lab.

My research focuses on the robustness and interpretability of large language models, with a particular emphasis on factuality, hallucinations, and the nature of the knowledge they acquire. I develop rigorous methods for evaluating model robustness, investigate the roots of their failures, and explore ways to make them more reliable and trustworthy.

Education

I hold a BA in Economics from the HSE, an MA in Economics from NES, and an MSc in Mathematics and Computer Science from Skoltech, where I graduated summa cum laude.

Selected Publications

LLM-Independent Adaptive RAG: Let the Question Speak for Itself

Maria Marina, Nikolay Ivanov, Sergey Pletenev, Mikhail Salnikov, Daria Galimzianova, Nikita Krayko, Vasily Konovalov, Alexander Panchenko, Viktor Moskvoretskii

EMNLP 2025

LLMs can sound confident even when they are wrong, and while RAG helps reduce hallucinations, it often adds heavy computational cost and can still surface misleading information. We introduce a lightweight, LLM-independent approach to adaptive retrieval that uses external signals to decide when retrieval is actually needed. Our method matches the accuracy of more complex LLM-based systems while delivering substantial efficiency gains — showing that smarter retrieval does not have to be heavier.

Will It Still Be True Tomorrow? Multilingual Evergreen Question Classification to Improve Trustworthy QA

Sergey Pletenev*, Maria Marina*, Nikolay Ivanov, Daria Galimzianova, Nikita Krayko, Mikhail Salnikov, Vasily Konovalov, Alexander Panchenko, Viktor Moskvoretskii

EMNLP 2025

We study whether a largely overlooked factor behind LLM hallucinations in QA is the temporality of the question itself: whether its answer is stable over time or likely to change. We introduce EverGreenQA, the first multilingual dataset for evergreen question classification, and show that modern LLMs do not reliably capture this distinction, either through explicit judgments or uncertainty signals. We then train EG-E5, a lightweight classifier that reaches state-of-the-art performance, and show that evergreen classification improves self-knowledge estimation, helps filter QA benchmarks, and strongly explains GPT-4o’s retrieval behavior.

Beyond Detection: Rethinking Education in the Age of AI-Writing

Maria Marina, Alexander Panchenko, Vasily Konovalov

AIED 2025, Late Breaking Results

As generative AI tools like ChatGPT enter classrooms, workplaces and everyday thinking, writing is at risk of becoming a formality – outsourced, automated and stripped of its cognitive value. But writing is not just output; it is how we learn to think. This paper explores what is lost when we let machines write for us, drawing on cognitive psychology, educational theory and real classroom practices. We argue that the process of writing – messy, slow, often frustrating – is where human deep learning happens. The paper also explores the current possibilities of AI-text detection, how educators can adapt through smarter pedagogy rather than bans, and why the ability to recognize machine-generated language may become a critical literacy of the 21st century. In a world where writing can be faked, learning cannot.

How Much Knowledge Can You Pack into a LoRA Adapter without Harming LLM?

Sergey Pletenev*, Maria Marina*, Daniil Moskovskiy, Vasily Konovalov, Pavel Braslavski, Alexander Panchenko, Mikhail Salnikov

NAACL 2025, Findings

We study how much new factual knowledge can be injected into an LLM through a LoRA adapter without damaging what the model already knows. We show that LoRA learns best from a mix of known and new facts, but even then, updating the model can hurt performance on external QA benchmarks and push it toward a few overrepresented answers. Our findings expose a sharp trade-off between packing in new knowledge and preserving general capabilities.
